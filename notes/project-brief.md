# Project Brief

## Core Question

Are balanced clause pairs disproportionately associated with comparison relations in naturally occurring rhetorical prose?

## Provisional Estimand

Among adjacent clause pairs with comparable syntactic opportunity for parallelism, estimate the difference in graded isocolon score between comparison relations and matched non-comparison relations.

## Primary Study

The first study should be a matched corpus study. Clause pairs are coded for discourse relation and for formal balance. The model asks whether comparison predicts balance after controlling for text, genre, author or speaker where known, length, connective type, syntactic frame, and general parallelism.

## Secondary Study

The second study should use revision or production evidence where available. The target question is whether writers add, preserve, or intensify isocolon when sharpening comparative relations from draft to final form.

## Gelman-Style Workflow

1. Define the estimand before collecting data.
2. Treat isocolon as a continuous noisy measure, not a brittle binary.
3. Treat discourse-relation labels as uncertain.
4. Simulate fake data before hand-coding at scale.
5. Use multilevel models with partial pooling by text, genre, and author or speaker where known.
6. Plot raw distributions and within-text contrasts before interpreting model summaries.

## Claim Ladder

Weak claim: Isocolon occurs near comparison language.

Useful claim: Isocolon is more frequent in comparison relations than in matched non-comparison relations.

Strong production-side claim: Writers select isocolon when making comparison rhetorically sharper.

Do not claim: Isocolon causes readers to perceive comparison as more forceful.
