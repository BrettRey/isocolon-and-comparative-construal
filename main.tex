\begin{abstract}
Rhetorical theory often treats balanced form as more than ornament. A
balanced pair of cola can present two units as candidates for
comparison. A reader-side causal claim would need experimental evidence,
but a production-side textual claim can be tested in corpus data. This
paper tests whether adjacent discourse-relation pairs in the Georgetown
University Multilayer corpus with enhanced Rhetorical Structure Theory
annotations (GUM/eRST) show more \term{formal balance} when they construe two
units as comparable co-members of a set. The analysis separates
\term{isocolon}, measured as length balance, from \term{parison},
measured as syntactic parallelism, and \term{lexical echo}. In 17,456 adjacent
relation pairs from 299 documents across 24 genres, a broad comparison
and co-ordination family shows a positive adjusted increase in composite
formal balance. The largest broad-family effects come from lists and
disjunctions, where co-ordination is a strong grammatical null; the
cleaner non-enumerative signal comes from adversative contrast.

The composite increase comes mostly from parison. Mean-only length controls
also show length balance, but they contribute little to the composite
signal. A matched co-ordination check shrinks the list/disjunction effect;
an adversative-only check leaves contrast positive. Antithesis alone sits
near zero, and concession is negative. The result supports
a restrained textual claim: formal balance belongs to the profile of some
comparable-set construals and varies across relations that could be called
contrastive.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Rhetorical theory has long treated figures as patterned forms with
argumentative force. In that tradition,
\term{isocolon} joins two kinds of evidence that are easy to confuse:
formal balance between adjacent units and a reader's sense that the units
invite comparison. The motivating claim is simple. When writers place two
clauses or phrases in balanced form, they may present the comparison
between them as more explicit, pointed, or forceful.

This paper asks how much of that claim can be tested without a reader
experiment. The answer depends on the explanatory level. At the
reader-response level, the claim says that isocolon changes what readers
notice or how forcefully they construe a relation. Corpus evidence alone
can't show that. At the textual level, the claim turns distributional:
writers may preferentially use balanced form where they construe two
units as comparable. That claim is testable.

The corpus test uses a narrow hypothesis. When a relation places two
adjacent units in a shared comparison frame, the pair should show more
formal balance than pairs expressing cause, condition, elaboration,
sequence, or other non-comparative relations. The study uses the
Georgetown University Multilayer (GUM) corpus and enhanced Rhetorical
Structure Theory (eRST) annotations because they provide genre-diverse
texts, discourse-relation labels, and signal annotation
\citep{zeldes2017GUMCorpus,zeldesEtAl2025eRST}.

The analysis uses the eRST relation export: mostly GUM, plus a smaller
GENTLE challenge set included in the same export. Access to the
Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST) Signalling Corpus is still pending, so
that corpus isn't part of this analysis. Once available, it can support later
triangulation against a broader inventory of coherence-relation signals.

The pattern refines the rhetorical claim. Broadly comparative and
co-ordinative relations are more formally balanced than matched
non-target relations, but the largest broad-family effects occur exactly
where a grammar-driven co-ordination account is strongest: lists and
disjunctions. Ordinary adversative contrast is smaller but cleaner,
because it survives inside an adversative-only comparison. Antithesis
alone is near zero, and concession is negative. The pattern suggests that
formal balance, especially parison-like syntactic parallelism, tracks
some acts of comparative construal better than it tracks the inherited
relation name \term{antithesis}.

\section{Rhetorical figures as form-function pairings}

The analysis starts from Fahnestock's account of rhetorical
figures as form-function pairings. In her work on scientific argument and
rhetorical style, figures are recurrent formal arrangements that can
epitomize lines of
reasoning \citep{fahnestock1999RhetoricalFiguresScience,fahnestock2011RhetoricalStyle}.
That view gives the corpus prediction. If a figure
serves an argumentative operation, then the figure should be
distributed unevenly across the discourse contexts where that operation
is being performed.

Fahnestock's discussion of the figures of argument speaks directly to
this project. \term{Isocolon} concerns approximate equality in the
length of adjacent cola, while \term{parison} concerns similarity of
grammatical structure.

The two often co-occur, but the properties differ.
A pair can be close in length without being syntactically
parallel, and it can be syntactically parallel without being closely
balanced in length. The classical tradition links both to comparison and
contrast because similar form can make two units cohere as an
argumentative set \citep{fahnestock2011RhetoricalStyle}.\footnote{I
approach these terms as a corpus linguist with recent rhetorical reading.
That outsider position matters: the Greek terms motivate hypotheses, but
the corpus tests the mappings. Less formally, this study stays suspicious of
its own Greek.}

A figure can help make a relation salient without being
obligatory whenever the relation is present. For the corpus test, uneven
distribution is expected, and frequency by itself gives no direct measure
of the rhetorical value of the figure.

The same point motivates computational work on rhetorical figures.
\textcite{harrisDimarco2017RhetoricalFiguresComputation} argue that
figural form is often iconically related to discourse function, so
surface patterning can provide evidence about pragmatic and argumentative
actions. \textcite{harrisEtAl2018AnnotationSchemeRhetoricalFigures}
develop an annotation scheme for figures in part because figure
identification requires attention to both formal regularity and
rhetorical role. \textcite{lawrenceEtAl2017HarnessingRhetoricalFigures}
make the parallel point for argument mining: rhetorical figures can
identify regions where argumentative work is being done, but only if
formal detection is connected to the content and relation being advanced.

Together, these studies motivate the working assumption. The analysis
pairs surface balance with a discourse relation that makes two units
available for comparison. The operational question asks whether formal
balance, separated into isocolonic length balance, parison-like syntactic
parallelism, and lexical echo, is higher in the relations where
rhetorical theory predicts that balance has value.

\section{From candidate form to rhetorical function}

The primary methodological challenge concerns over-attribution. Repetition and
parallelism are common in ordinary text, and only some instances have the
rhetorical force that figure theory cares about. Work on automatic
chiasmus detection makes the problem vivid. \textcite{dubremetzNivre2018FigureDetection}
show that trivial repetition patterns are easy to find, but repetitions
with rhetorical effect are much harder to separate from accidental or
irrelevant patterns. They treat figure detection as a ranking
problem instead of a clean binary classification problem. The problem is
not finding parallelism. The problem is persuading it to mean something.

That lesson carries over to isocolon and parison. Exact repetition, list formatting,
dialogue formulas, quoted slogans, and segmentation artifacts can all
look formally balanced. Some are rhetorically relevant, but many aren't
instances of the target phenomenon. Recent survey work makes the broader
point: rhetorical figure detection remains limited by small datasets,
inconsistent definitions, and over-reliance on rule-based surface cues
\citep{kuehnMitrovic2024ElephantRoom,kuehnEtAl2024LesserKnownRhetoricalFigures}. A credible corpus
test should use surface scores cautiously, validate them
against human judgment, and avoid treating high balance as equivalent to
a gold figure label.

The analysis below follows that strategy. The composite formal-balance
score is a continuous outcome in the distributional analysis and a
ranking score in validation. It ranks adjacent relation pairs by how much
balance they display. The qualitative audit then asks whether
high-scoring examples are plausible rhetorical-balance candidates after
excluding exact repetition, dialogue formulas, and other false-positive
types. This more modest target than automatic figure recognition is the
right target for a first rhetorical-function test.

\section{Corpus and measurement}

The corpus comes from the English Georgetown University Multilayer corpus
with enhanced Rhetorical Structure Theory annotations (GUM/eRST). GUM
provides genre-diverse, richly annotated English texts
\citep{zeldes2017GUMCorpus}. eRST extends RST-style discourse annotation
with graph structures and explicit signal annotation across spoken and
written genres \citep{zeldesEtAl2025eRST}. The DISRPT shared-task export
used here also includes a smaller GENTLE challenge set. The project needs
discourse relations as well as adjacent sentences. It also needs enough
genre variation to separate a rhetorical effect from a register-specific
habit.

The analysis uses adjacent discourse-relation pairs. The dataset
contains 17,456 such pairs from 299 documents across 24 genres.
These discourse units serve as candidate cola-like spans, with no claim
that they're classical cola in a strict prosodic sense.

A relation enters the analysis if eRST marks it as explicit or implicit,
both spans have token intervals, and the two intervals are adjacent in
document order. Non-adjacent linked spans are excluded. Concurrent graph
relations are kept as pairwise relation rows when their spans meet this
adjacency criterion.

The DISRPT shared-task relation export used here omits the full eRST graph-node
inventory from the derived table. The diagnostics in
Table~\ref{tab:extraction-diagnostics} are interval-level checks instead
of a complete graph-theoretic audit. The extracted set contains 16,278
rows with collection label GUM and 1,178 with collection label
\mention{GENTLE}, the out-of-domain challenge set; the 24 genres are the
document-genre labels in the extracted files.

For each pair, I computed three surface measures. First,
\term{isocolon} measures length balance over token, character,
and estimated syllable length. Second, \term{parison} measures
similarity in dependency and lexical-category profiles. Third,
\term{lexical echo} captures repeated or closely corresponding lexical
material. A composite score combines the three as a graded
measure of formal balance. The score gives evidence about balance, not a
figure label for the pair.

The component scoring stays deliberately simple. For any two units with positive length,
a balance component is \(1 - |n_1 - n_2|/\max(n_1,n_2)\). The length
score averages this quantity over word tokens, characters, and estimated
syllables. The syntax score averages normalized edit similarity over
Universal Dependencies \mention{UPOS} lexical-category tags,
language-specific \mention{XPOS} lexical-category tags, and
dependency-relation sequences read in token order. I treat this as a
surface syntactic profile, not a tree-alignment measure. The lexical
score averages lemma-sequence edit similarity and Jaccard overlap over
content lemmas.

The GUM/eRST files use the Conference on Natural Language Learning format
for Universal Dependencies (CoNLL-U), which records tokenization, lemmas,
lexical-category tags, and dependency labels. Punctuation tokens are
excluded from the word, syntax, and lexical sequences, while colon and
semicolon position are kept as separate controls. Syllable counts are
approximate vowel-group counts.

The composite equals
\[
0.40 \times \text{length} +
0.45 \times \text{syntax} +
0.15 \times \text{lexical}.
\]

These weights define only the composite formal-balance score. The
components are also reported separately, so the analysis can distinguish
isocolonic length balance from parison-like syntactic parallelism.

The target relations were defined before the final robustness checks as
analysis groups, separate from a new annotation layer. Table~\ref{tab:label-mapping}
gives the mapping. The broad target family includes
\mention{adversative-antithesis}, \mention{adversative-contrast},
\mention{joint-disjunction}, and \mention{joint-list}. This family tests
whether formal balance rises in relations that place units in a
comparable or co-ordinated frame. The narrow target family includes only
\mention{adversative-antithesis} and \mention{adversative-contrast}.
Individual-relation analyses separate the broad effect into its component
labels.

The primary comparisons ask a plain difference question: after removing
obvious confounds, do target relations receive higher balance scores than
other relations? The controls remove differences that can make the
comparison too easy: document, explicit signalling, sentence adjacency,
punctuation, mean length, and maximum length. Document controls mean that,
where possible, target and non-target pairs are compared inside the same
text.

For readability, I report effects as score-point differences on a 0--100
version of the balance scale. A difference of 4.1 points means that target
pairs score about four points higher than comparable non-target pairs
after the controls. I estimate those differences with ordinary least
squares, a regression method for average differences. The residualized
version used here first removes the controls from both the target marker
and the outcome, then compares what remains.

For the composite score, the controls matter because longer spans have
more opportunities for structural mismatch. For the length subscore, the
pair of length controls overcontrols the target quantity: token-length
balance is \(2\mu/m - 1\), where \(\mu\) is mean span length and \(m\) is
maximum span length. The isocolon subscore is reported with mean-length
control and without maximum-length control.

The main controls are an intercept, explicit relation status,
same-sentence adjacency, colon punctuation, semicolon punctuation,
standardized log mean length, standardized log maximum length, and
document fixed effects. Document-clustered intervals treat pairs from the
same document as related observations rather than as wholly independent
pieces of evidence.

A genre-varying analysis asks whether the effect belongs to only one or
two genres. It first estimates the same adjusted effect within each genre,
then uses a normal empirical-Bayes model. In plain terms, that model lets
noisy genre estimates borrow strength from the overall pattern while still
estimating how much genres vary around the shared mean.

Robustness checks add mean-only length models, joint-only and
adversative-only comparisons, GUM-only and continuous-span-only subsets,
leave-one-genre influence checks, score-weight sensitivity checks, and
stratified permutation checks. In the permutation checks, the target
labels are shuffled inside matched strata. The question is whether effects
this large appear when relation labels are randomly assigned within
comparable document, length, punctuation, and explicitness groups.

\section{Validation}

The validation asks whether the score ranks useful cases near the top. It
doesn't ask whether the score can label figures automatically. The eRST
\mention{syn-prl} signal labels mark cases where annotators identified
syntactic parallelism as a discourse signal. Those labels give a small
check on formal parallelism, not on rhetorical force.

I report the area under the receiver-operating characteristic curve (AUC)
for its simple ranking interpretation. If one labelled
\mention{syn-prl} pair and one unlabelled pair are drawn at random, AUC is
the chance that the score ranks the labelled pair higher. A value of 0.5
means chance ranking; a value of 1.0 means perfect ranking. The full
receiver-operating characteristic curve plots recall against false-positive
rate at every possible cutoff, but the ranking interpretation is the point
needed here.

On that scale, the composite score has an AUC of 0.73, with an
approximate Hanley--McNeil interval from 0.66 to 0.80. The parison
subscore is slightly higher at 0.75 [0.69, 0.82], lexical echo is
0.72 [0.65, 0.79], and isocolonic length balance is 0.65 [0.58,
0.72] (Table~\ref{tab:score-validation}). The interval gives a rough
uncertainty range for the AUC, not a new effect size.

The threshold numbers say how useful the ranked list would be for audit.
Precision asks how many of the top-scoring pairs carry a \mention{syn-prl}
label. Recall asks how many of all \mention{syn-prl} pairs appear in that
top slice. Lift compares the precision to the label's overall prevalence.
At the 95th percentile, precision reaches only 3\%, but recall reaches
37\%, a lift of 7.4. At the 97.5th percentile, precision rises to 5.5\%,
recall reaches 33\%, and lift reaches 13.1.

Those numbers look poor for automatic labelling but adequate for ranking
rare candidates for audit. The validation also stays narrow: the
\mention{syn-prl} labels validate a surface parallelism signal. In this
implementation, a pair is positive if either unit contains a token-level
\mention{syn-prl} signal in the eRST export, so the validation uses
span-level signal presence as a proxy for relation-level syntactic
parallelism. It doesn't validate rhetorical function itself.

The qualitative audit supplies a pilot rhetorical-function check. I coded
80 candidate pairs in a single-author, non-blind audit; row strata and
scores were visible. Automated reviews only flagged borderline cases after
coding. The audit isn't interrater-reliability evidence. It identifies
false-positive classes and illustrative examples.

The pattern is the point: high broad-target candidates usually survived
the audit, while repetition controls didn't. Exact repetition gives
strong surface balance, so it functions as a control rather than a target.

The audit also clarified the boundary of the category. Bare identity,
apposition, dialogue repair, and exact repetition can be rhetorically
relevant apart from isocolon. Conversely, weak length balance can
still matter when the two units share a frame strongly enough to invite
slot substitution. The coding decisions treat isocolon as a
component of a broader rhetorical-balance judgment, with false-positive
classes instead of a mechanically recoverable surface pattern.

\section{Results}

The broad target family is more formally balanced than the comparison
set. On a 0--100 version of the composite score, target pairs are 4.1
points higher after controls. The document-clustered interval runs from
3.6 to 4.7 points. That shift is modest but stable: about 0.28 standard
deviations of the observed composite score.

The components show what kind of balance carries the effect. The parison
difference is 7.7 points, compared with 2.4 points for lexical echo. With
mean-length control only, the broad isocolon difference is 6.6 points.
Table~\ref{tab:relation-effects} reports the composite and the three
components separately.

The broad-family result is an omnibus check. Its strongest labels are also
the labels where co-ordination makes parallel form easiest, so the cleaner
rhetorical test comes from more focused comparisons. The narrow adversative
family, \mention{adversative-antithesis} plus
\mention{adversative-contrast}, shows a smaller 1.3-point composite
difference, with an interval from 0.5 to 2.1 points.

The individual labels explain the difference.
\mention{joint-disjunction} has an adjusted formal-balance effect of
4.8 points, \mention{joint-list} has an adjusted effect of 4.4 points, and
\mention{adversative-contrast} has the smaller but more diagnostic
effect of 2.4 points.
\mention{adversative-antithesis} is near zero at 0.4 points, with an
interval that crosses zero. I read this as a mismatch between a
discourse-relation label and the classical figure. It gives no evidence
against antithesis as a rhetorical resource.
\mention{adversative-concession} is negative at -1.3 points.

The relation-level scan also identifies an important positive control.
\mention{restatement-repetition} has the strongest adjusted composite
effect, 19.0 points, with especially large lexical and parison effects.
That's exactly what a surface score should find: exact identity is highly
balanced, with lexical echo stronger than the syntactic effect. The human
audit shows why it needs separate treatment.
Repetition can signal clarification, identity, repair, emphasis, or other
rhetorical work; exact repetition remains distinct from balanced
comparison. The analysis treats restatement/repetition as a control
and diagnostic, separate from the comparison hypothesis.

Genre-varying estimates point in the same direction. The broad target has
a partially pooled mean difference of 3.9 points across 20 genres. The
\mention{joint-list} estimate is 4.0 points across 17 genres, and
\mention{adversative-contrast} is 2.9 points across the genres where
there's enough evidence to estimate it. The narrow adversative estimate
is 1.2 points. The between-genre standard deviation is about 1.1 points
for the broad target, 1.3 for \mention{joint-list}, 0.9 for the narrow
adversative target, and 0.7 for \mention{adversative-contrast}. The broad
effect appears in multiple genres.

\subsection{Robustness checks}

The primary formal-balance results survive document-clustered uncertainty
estimates. With document clustering, the broad-family effect is 4.1
points, with an interval from 3.6 to 4.7. The \mention{joint-list} effect
is 4.4 [3.7, 5.1], \mention{joint-disjunction} is 4.8 [3.0, 6.7], and
\mention{adversative-contrast} is 2.4 [1.3, 3.5].
\mention{adversative-antithesis} remains near zero, 0.4 [-0.7, 1.5].

The length-balance result uses a different control specification. Mean
span length and maximum span length nearly determine the length-balance
score, so using both controls suppresses the quantity being estimated.
With mean-length control but without maximum-length control, the
broad-family effect on length balance is 6.6 [5.6, 7.7], and
\mention{adversative-contrast} is 7.3 [5.0, 9.6]. The corresponding
no-length-controls estimates are nearly identical: 6.7 points for the broad
family and 7.1 points for \mention{adversative-contrast}. The length-balance
claim is reported under mean-length control.

The obvious objection is the right one. Lists and disjunctions are easy
places to be parallel: they involve matched daughters, repeated frames, and
often explicit co-ordination. The joint-only comparison asks whether
\mention{joint-list} and \mention{joint-disjunction} survive against
other multinuclear relations. They do, but the effect shrinks to
1.7 points [0.6, 2.8]. The corresponding parison effect is 2.9 [1.0,
4.8], while the isocolon effect is only 0.3 [-0.7, 1.3].

The adversative-only comparison is cleaner. It compares
\mention{adversative-contrast} against other adversative relations, where
list syntax isn't the main explanation. Contrast remains positive at
3.0 [1.4, 4.7], again led by parison. The joint-only baseline
consists of \mention{joint-sequence} and \mention{joint-other}; that
baseline remains multinuclear and partly co-ordinative.

Two data-scope checks give similar results. In the GUM-only subset, the
broad-family effect is 4.0 [3.4, 4.6], the within-joint list/disjunction
effect is 1.7 [0.6, 2.9], and the within-adversative contrast effect is
3.4 [1.6, 5.1]. Excluding the 1,963 adjacent rows with discontinuous token
intervals leaves the broad-family effect at 4.5 [3.8, 5.1], the
within-joint list/disjunction effect at 1.8 [0.6, 3.0], and the
within-adversative contrast effect at 3.3 [1.4, 5.2]. A leave-one-genre
check also stays stable: the broad-family estimate ranges from 4.0 to
4.3, the within-joint estimate from 1.5 to 2.0, and the
within-adversative contrast estimate from 2.8 to 3.2.

The stratified permutation check shuffled target labels
500 times within strata defined by document, explicitness,
same-sentence status, colon punctuation, semicolon punctuation, and
quartile of mean word length. The model specification was otherwise the
same as in the main composite analysis.

This partial null doesn't match local phrase type or root dependency
category. It asks whether the result follows from target-label allocation
across documents, lengths, punctuation, and explicitness. The observed
broad-family effect, the
list/disjunction effect, and the adversative-contrast effect all fall
above the 97.5th percentile of their shuffled null distributions
(Table~\ref{tab:stratified-null}). In 500 stratified shuffles, none of
the shuffled label assignments produced an effect as large as the observed
one. Because 500 shuffles give only about 0.002 resolution in the tail, I
treat this as a calibration check rather than as a precise \(p\) value.
The point isn't family-wise significance testing; it's that the observed
effects sit well outside the label-allocation null.

The composite result stays stable across the hand-set weights. Across 66
grid-based nonnegative weightings of length, syntax, and lexical echo,
the broad-family effect has a positive interval in every
specification. For \mention{adversative-contrast}, 65 of 66 grid
specifications are positive and 59 have positive intervals.

\section{Interpretation}

The findings support a restricted version of the rhetorical-function
claim. I treat balanced form as a selective way of presenting
units as comparable. It's optional even when comparison is present. That
view predicts an uneven distribution: balance should cluster where
matched presentation has rhetorical value, while remaining absent in many ordinary
cases. The corpus result fits that version, but unevenly. Lists and
disjunctions supply the largest broad-family effects; adversative
contrast supplies the cleaner non-enumerative case.

\term{Comparable set} works here as a descriptive cover term for co-membership
relations, including bare co-enumeration. The term allows list structure
without treating every list as comparison for its own sake. The cleanest
non-enumerative signal in the analysis comes from
\mention{adversative-contrast}.

The component results then refine the claim. The dominant effect looks
parison-like more than isocolonic in the narrow length-balance sense. The
evidence weakens for labels that look rhetorically relevant in the
taxonomy but don't necessarily require balanced presentation in the text.

In particular, \mention{adversative-antithesis} as an eRST relation label
only partly overlaps with antithesis in the classical theory of figures.
The label \mention{adversative-antithesis} keeps the name while losing
the balance.\footnote{The sentence is itself a small \term{isocolon} and
\term{parison}, and it asserts an \term{antithesis}. Either the author is
demonstrating the apparatus or the apparatus is demonstrating the
author.} The conservative conclusion leaves antithesis itself intact:
the corpus label \mention{adversative-antithesis} serves as a poor direct proxy
for the classical figure.

That point remains a limitation as well as a finding. The 80-pair audit
contains only eight rows with the \mention{adversative-antithesis} label.
These rows support the label-mismatch interpretation, but they're too few
to separate label mismatch from a second possibility: genuine semantic
antitheses may be expressed through opposition instead of lexical
overlap or surface parallelism.

The subset test is defined but not yet reported here. The follow-up
worksheet covers all 343 eRST \mention{adversative-antithesis} rows and
asks for three judgments: classical antithesis, semantic opposition, and
parallel opposition. Once those fields are coded, the analysis compares
yes and no rows on formal balance, isocolon, parison, and lexical echo.
Until then, the near-zero \mention{adversative-antithesis} result is
evidence about the corpus label, not a settled test of classical
antithesis itself.

The composite formal-balance score tracks a broader act of
placing units into a shared frame. That
frame can be contrastive, but it can also be co-ordinative, disjunctive,
or list-like. In the latter cases, co-ordination itself favours matched
daughters and weakens the rhetorical interpretation. Conversely, some
adversative relations have little balance
because their function is concession, qualification, or discourse
management instead of matched presentation.

Corpus data record ordinary production, not an ideal of trained
rhetorical practice. The evidence shows where balance appears in
this corpus. It doesn't show where balanced form could improve a text.
The language scope is also narrow. These are English corpus data, mostly
from GUM, with English word order, English morphology, English punctuation,
and English written and spoken segmentation. Nothing here licenses a
claim about how isocolon or parison behave in Ancient Greek, Modern
Japanese, or any other language whose prosody, morphology, discourse-unit
segmentation, or rhetorical training traditions may differ.

The co-ordination caveat matters. Lists and disjunctions are
places where parallel syntax is grammatically cheap, so the broad effect
can't be read as a pure rhetorical choice. The joint-only robustness
check shrinks the effect, which is what that objection predicts. The
effect remains positive, and \mention{adversative-contrast} remains
positive inside the adversative family and under a stratified permutation
null.

The safer interpretation says that comparable-set relations attract
formal balance beyond genre, document, length, and punctuation
differences, while much of that balance follows the syntax of
co-ordination. Relation labels don't guarantee classical figures, and
classical figures needn't appear where relation labels promise them.

The pattern also clarifies the scope of the corpus study. The evidence
stays textual and distributional. It concerns where balanced form occurs, not
how readers experience those pairs. A high score by itself still falls
short of a rhetorical-figure label. The analysis shows that a graded
measure of formal balance is systematically
higher in particular discourse-relation contexts. The human audit
illustrates why many high-scoring cases are rhetorically relevant and why
this remains pilot evidence, pending an independently validated
annotation layer.

The RST Signalling Corpus isn't part of the analysis yet because access is
still pending. Once available, that corpus can add a later triangulation
point.

Work on discourse signalling has shown that coherence
relations are often signalled by more than discourse markers, including
lexical, syntactic, graphical, semantic, and genre-based cues. That point
is developed in the original signalling annotation work and in later
studies of RST signals and multiple signals
\citep{taboadaDas2013AnnotationUponAnnotation,dasTaboada2018RSTSignallingCorpus,dasTaboada2018SignallingBeyondMarkers,dasTaboada2019MultipleSignals}.
This literature gives the project a stronger comparative baseline. If
formal balance behaves like a discourse signal, then it should be
testable alongside other signal types as well as an isolated figure
score.

\section{Conclusion}

A corpus study can't prove that isocolon makes readers perceive
comparison as more forceful, and it leaves rhetorical pedagogy untested.
It can test a cleaner textual claim: writers use formal balance unevenly
across discourse relations, and the relations most associated with
comparable-set construals should show more of it. These results
support that claim for a broad family of comparison-like and
co-ordinative relations, with the largest effects in lists and
disjunctions and the cleaner non-enumerative effect in adversative
contrast.

The analysis gives selective support to the inherited figure label
\term{antithesis}. The narrow adversative effect stays small, and antithesis
as a discourse-relation label sits close to zero. That finding sharpens the
mapping between relation labels and classical figures. The strongest
signal comes from parison-like parallelism; isocolon narrowly understood as
length balance contributes less to the main composite effect. Balanced
form remains a rhetorical resource, and the
corpus evidence supports a claim about comparable-set construals
and formal balance more securely than a claim about equal cola alone.

The robustness checks sharpen that conclusion. Length balance appears
under mean-length conditioning without maximum length, while parison leads
the main composite model. The joint-only comparison also shows
that a large share of the broad effect reflects co-ordination itself. The
stratified permutation check places the observed effects above document,
length, punctuation, and explicitness-matched nulls. The result maps
where figural form begins to do work.
