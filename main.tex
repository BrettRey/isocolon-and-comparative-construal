\begin{abstract}
Rhetorical theory often treats \term{isocolon} as more than ornamental
balance. A balanced pair of clauses can make a comparison feel more
explicit, pointed, or forceful. A reader-side causal claim would need
experimental evidence, but a production-side claim can be tested in
corpus data. This paper proposes a narrow test: among adjacent clause
pairs with comparable syntactic opportunity for parallelism, are
comparison relations more isocolonic than contrast, cause, condition,
sequence, or elaboration relations? The design treats isocolon as a
graded textual property rather than a binary label, models clause pairs
within texts and genres, and separates comparative relation from the
parallel syntax that comparison often invites. If the association
survives those controls, the result would support a textual-function
claim: isocolon is preferentially selected where writers construe two
units as comparable.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

At Rhetoricon, held at the University of Waterloo on 2026-05-29, Gini
Fahnestock proposed that \term{isocolon} makes comparison more salient,
pointed, explicit, or forceful. The question for this project is how far
a corpus study can test that claim without turning into a reader
experiment.

Fahnestock's broader work treats rhetorical figures as forms that do
argumentative work rather than as detachable ornament
\citep{fahnestock1999RhetoricalFiguresScience,fahnestock2011RhetoricalStyle}.
That makes the corpus question worth asking, but it also makes
overclaiming easy.

The answer has to be level-specific. At the reader-response level, the
claim is causal: isocolon changes how a comparison is perceived. A
corpus study can't establish that. At the corpus level, the claim is
different: writers may preferentially use balanced clauses when
construing two units as comparable. That claim is testable.

\section{Target Claim}

The first target is deliberately narrow. In naturally occurring
rhetorical prose, isocolon should be more frequent in comparison
relations than in matched non-comparison relations, once genre, text,
clause length, syntactic frame, connective type, and general parallelism
are controlled.

That would not show that readers experience the comparison as more
forceful. The result would show that formal balance is part of the
textual profile of comparative construal. The rhetorical-function claim
would then rest on production-side textual evidence rather than
psychometric response data.

\section{Design}

The unit of analysis is the adjacent clause pair or colon pair. Each pair
receives a discourse-relation label, with comparison contrasted against
contrast, cause, condition, temporal sequence, and elaboration. Each pair
also receives a graded isocolon score, combining token-length balance,
syllable or stress balance where feasible, syntactic parallelism, and
lexical parallelism.

The main model should estimate whether comparison predicts higher
isocolon scores after partial pooling by text, author or speaker where
known, genre, and period. The analysis should treat the isocolon measure
and the discourse-relation labels as noisy. A fake-data simulation should
come first, to check whether a realistically sized hand-coded corpus
could recover an effect if one existed.

\section{Second Test}

A second non-experimental test would strengthen the inference. Revision
data, where available, can ask whether writers add or preserve isocolon
when sharpening comparison. If draft-to-final changes increase balance
specifically in comparative pairs, then isocolon is not merely a
by-product of comparison syntax. It's a selected feature of rhetorical
shaping.

The two tests would converge on different parts of the claim. The
matched corpus study asks whether isocolon is associated with comparison
relations. The revision study asks whether writers select isocolon while
making a comparison rhetorically sharper.

\section{Conclusion}

The project should not claim that isocolon causes readers to perceive
comparison as more forceful. It can make a stronger and cleaner textual
claim: isocolon is a conventional formal resource for marking comparative
construal. If that claim holds under matched corpus and revision tests,
the production-side evidence would be substantial.
