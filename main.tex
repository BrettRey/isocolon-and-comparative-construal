\begin{abstract}
Rhetorical theory often treats balanced form as more than ornament. A
balanced pair of cola can present two units as candidates for
comparison. A reader-side causal claim would need experimental evidence,
but a production-side textual claim can be tested in corpus data. This
paper tests whether adjacent discourse-relation pairs in the Georgetown
University Multilayer corpus with enhanced Rhetorical Structure Theory
annotations (GUM/eRST) show more \term{formal balance} when they construe two
units as comparable co-members of a set. The analysis separates
\term{isocolon}, measured as length balance, from \term{parison},
measured as syntactic parallelism, and \term{lexical echo}. In 17,456 adjacent
relation pairs from 299 documents across 24 genres, a broad comparison
and co-ordination family shows a positive adjusted increase in composite
formal balance. The largest broad-family effects come from lists and
disjunctions, where co-ordination is a strong grammatical null; the
cleaner non-enumerative signal comes from adversative contrast.

The composite increase is mostly parison-like. Mean-only length controls
also show length-balance effects, but those effects are not the main
source of the composite signal. A matched co-ordination check shrinks the
list/disjunction effect; an adversative-only check leaves contrast
positive. Antithesis alone is near zero, and concession is negative. The
result supports a restrained textual claim: formal balance is part of the
profile of some comparable-set construals and varies across relations
that could be called contrastive.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Rhetorical theory has long treated figures as patterned forms with
argumentative force. In that tradition,
\term{isocolon} joins two kinds of evidence that are easy to confuse:
formal balance between adjacent units and a reader's sense that the units
invite comparison. The motivating claim is simple. When writers place two
clauses or phrases in balanced form, they may present the comparison
between them as more explicit, pointed, or forceful.

This paper asks how much of that claim can be tested without a reader
experiment. The answer depends on the explanatory level. At the
reader-response level, the claim is causal: isocolon changes what readers
notice or how forcefully they construe a relation. Corpus evidence alone
can't show that. At the textual level, the claim is distributional:
writers may preferentially use balanced form where they construe two
units as comparable. That claim is testable.

The corpus test uses a narrower hypothesis. When a relation places two
adjacent units in a shared comparison frame, the pair should show more
formal balance than pairs expressing cause, condition, elaboration,
sequence, or other non-comparative relations. The study uses the
Georgetown University Multilayer (GUM) corpus and enhanced Rhetorical
Structure Theory (eRST) annotations because they provide genre-diverse
texts, discourse-relation labels, and signal annotation
\citep{zeldes2017GUMCorpus,zeldesEtAl2025eRST}.
The present analysis gives a first distributional test using GUM/eRST.
The Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST) Signalling Corpus will allow later
triangulation against a broader inventory of coherence-relation signals.

The pattern refines the rhetorical claim. Broadly comparative and
co-ordinative relations are more formally balanced than matched
non-target relations, but the largest broad-family effects occur exactly
where a grammar-driven co-ordination account is strongest: lists and
disjunctions. Ordinary adversative contrast is smaller but cleaner,
because it survives inside an adversative-only comparison. Antithesis
alone is near zero, and concession is negative. The pattern suggests that
formal balance, especially parison-like syntactic parallelism, tracks
some acts of comparative construal better than it tracks the inherited
relation name \term{antithesis}.

\section{Rhetorical figures as form-function pairings}

The theoretical starting point is Fahnestock's account of rhetorical
figures as form-function pairings. In her work on scientific argument and
rhetorical style, figures are recurrent formal arrangements that can
epitomize lines of
reasoning \citep{fahnestock1999RhetoricalFiguresScience,fahnestock2011RhetoricalStyle}.
That view gives the corpus prediction. If a figure
is tied to an argumentative operation, then the figure should be
distributed unevenly across the discourse contexts where that operation
is being performed.

Fahnestock's discussion of the figures of argument is especially direct
for this project. \term{Isocolon} concerns approximate equality in the
length of adjacent cola, while \term{parison} concerns similarity of
grammatical structure. The two often co-occur, but the properties differ.
A pair can be close in length without being syntactically
parallel, and it can be syntactically parallel without being closely
balanced in length. The classical tradition links both to comparison and
contrast because similar form can make two units cohere as an
argumentative set \citep{fahnestock2011RhetoricalStyle}.

A figure can be a resource for making a relation salient without being
obligatory whenever the relation is present. For the corpus test, uneven
distribution is expected, and frequency by itself gives no direct measure
of the rhetorical value of the figure.

The same point motivates computational work on rhetorical figures.
\textcite{harrisDimarco2017RhetoricalFiguresComputation} argue that
figural form is often iconically related to discourse function, so
surface patterning can provide evidence about pragmatic and argumentative
actions. \textcite{harrisEtAl2018AnnotationSchemeRhetoricalFigures}
develop an annotation scheme for figures in part because figure
identification requires attention to both formal regularity and
rhetorical role. \textcite{lawrenceEtAl2017HarnessingRhetoricalFigures}
make the parallel point for argument mining: rhetorical figures can
identify regions where argumentative work is being done, but only if
formal detection is connected to the content and relation being advanced.

Together, these studies support the central assumption of the present study.
The relevant object is the co-occurrence of surface balance with a
discourse relation that makes two units available for comparison. The
operational question is whether formal balance, separated into
isocolonic length balance, parison-like syntactic parallelism, and
lexical echo, is higher in the relations where rhetorical theory predicts
that balance has value.

\section{From candidate form to rhetorical function}

The primary methodological challenge is over-attribution. Repetition and
parallelism are common in ordinary text, and only some instances have the
rhetorical force that figure theory cares about. Work on automatic
chiasmus detection makes the problem vivid. \textcite{dubremetzNivre2018FigureDetection}
show that trivial repetition patterns are easy to find, but repetitions
with rhetorical effect are much harder to separate from accidental or
irrelevant patterns. They treat figure detection as a ranking
problem instead of a clean binary classification problem.

That lesson carries over to isocolon and parison. Exact repetition, list formatting,
dialogue formulas, quoted slogans, and segmentation artifacts can all
look formally balanced. Some are rhetorically relevant, but many are not
instances of the target phenomenon. Recent survey work makes the broader
point: rhetorical figure detection remains limited by small datasets,
inconsistent definitions, and over-reliance on rule-based surface cues
\citep{kuehnMitrovic2024ElephantRoom,kuehnEtAl2024LesserKnownRhetoricalFigures}. A credible corpus
test should use surface scores cautiously, validate them
against human judgment, and avoid treating high balance as equivalent to
a gold figure label.

The analysis below follows that strategy. The composite formal-balance
score is a continuous outcome in the distributional analysis and a
ranking score in validation. It ranks adjacent relation pairs by how much
balance they display. The qualitative audit then asks whether
high-scoring examples are plausible rhetorical-balance candidates after
excluding exact repetition, dialogue formulas, and other false-positive
types. This more modest target than automatic figure recognition is the
right target for a first rhetorical-function test.

\section{Corpus and measurement}

The corpus is the English Georgetown University Multilayer corpus with
enhanced Rhetorical Structure Theory annotations (GUM/eRST). GUM is a
genre-diverse corpus with richly annotated English texts
\citep{zeldes2017GUMCorpus}. eRST extends RST-style discourse annotation
with graph structures and explicit signal annotation across spoken and
written genres \citep{zeldesEtAl2025eRST}. The project needs discourse
relations as well as adjacent sentences. It also needs enough genre
variation to separate a rhetorical effect from a
register-specific habit.

The unit of analysis is an adjacent discourse-relation pair. The
dataset contains 17,456 such pairs from 299 documents across 24 genres.
These discourse units serve as candidate cola-like spans, with no claim
that they're classical cola in a strict prosodic sense.

A relation enters the analysis if eRST marks it as explicit or implicit,
both spans have token intervals, and the two intervals are adjacent in
document order. Non-adjacent linked spans are excluded. Concurrent graph
relations are kept as pairwise relation rows when their spans meet this
adjacency criterion.

The DISRPT relation export used here omits the full eRST graph-node
inventory from the derived table. The diagnostics in
Table~\ref{tab:extraction-diagnostics} are interval-level checks instead
of a complete graph-theoretic audit. The extracted set contains 16,278
rows with collection label GUM and 1,178 with collection label
\mention{GENTLE}, the out-of-domain challenge set; the 24 genres are the
document-genre labels in the extracted files.

For each pair, I computed three surface measures. The first is
\term{isocolon}, operationalized as length balance over token, character,
and estimated syllable length. The second is \term{parison},
operationalized as similarity in dependency and part-of-speech profiles.
The third is \term{lexical echo}, based on repeated or closely corresponding
lexical material. A composite score combines the three as a graded
measure of formal balance. The score is evidence about balance, not a
figure label for the pair.

The component scoring is deliberately simple. For any two units with positive length,
a balance component is \(1 - |n_1 - n_2|/\max(n_1,n_2)\). The length
score averages this quantity over word tokens, characters, and estimated
syllables. The syntax score averages normalized edit similarity over
universal part-of-speech (UPOS), language-specific part-of-speech (XPOS),
and dependency-relation sequences read in token order. I treat this as a
surface syntactic profile, not a tree-alignment measure. The lexical
score averages lemma-sequence edit similarity and Jaccard overlap over
content lemmas.

The GUM/eRST files use the Conference on Natural Language Learning format
for Universal Dependencies (CoNLL-U), which records tokenization, lemmas,
part-of-speech tags, and dependency labels. Punctuation tokens are
excluded from the word, syntax, and lexical sequences, while colon and
semicolon position are kept as separate controls. Syllable counts are
approximate vowel-group counts.

The composite is
\[
0.40 \times \text{length} +
0.45 \times \text{syntax} +
0.15 \times \text{lexical}.
\]

These weights define only the composite formal-balance score. The
components are also reported separately, so the analysis can distinguish
isocolonic length balance from parison-like syntactic parallelism.

The target relations were defined before the final robustness checks as
analysis groups, separate from a new annotation layer. Table~\ref{tab:label-mapping}
gives the mapping. The broad target family includes
\mention{adversative-antithesis}, \mention{adversative-contrast},
\mention{joint-disjunction}, and \mention{joint-list}. This family tests
whether formal balance rises in relations that place units in a
comparable or co-ordinated frame. The narrow target family includes only
\mention{adversative-antithesis} and \mention{adversative-contrast}.
Individual-relation analyses separate the broad effect into its component
labels.

The primary comparisons adjust for the most obvious structural confounds.
Where possible, pairs are compared within documents. The models also
include controls for explicit signalling, sentence adjacency,
punctuation, mean length, and maximum length. Those controls are needed
for the composite score because longer spans have more opportunities for
structural mismatch. For the length subscore, the pair of length controls
overcontrols the target quantity: token-length balance is \(2\mu/m - 1\),
where \(\mu\) is mean span length and \(m\) is maximum span length. The
isocolon subscore is reported with mean-length control and without
maximum-length control.

A genre-varying analysis then checks whether the effect is concentrated
in one or two genres or survives partial pooling across genres.

The adjusted estimates are ordinary least-squares target coefficients
computed by residualizing both the target indicator and the outcome
against the controls. The controls are an intercept, explicit relation
status, same-sentence adjacency, colon punctuation, semicolon
punctuation, standardized log mean length, standardized log maximum
length, and document fixed effects. The genre-varying analysis first fits
the same adjusted effect within each genre, then uses a normal
empirical-Bayes model with genre effects distributed around a shared mean
and between-genre standard deviation.

Robustness checks add uncertainty estimates clustered by document,
mean-only length-balance models, a joint-only comparison, an
adversative-only comparison, and score-weight sensitivity checks.

\section{Validation}

The composite score ranks candidates for audit. Against the small number
of eRST \mention{syn-prl} signal
labels in the dataset, the composite score has an area under the
receiver-operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.731, with an
approximate Hanley--McNeil interval from 0.664 to 0.797. The parison
subscore is slightly higher at 0.754 [0.689, 0.819], lexical echo is
0.719 [0.652, 0.786], and isocolonic length balance is 0.646 [0.577,
0.715] (Table~\ref{tab:score-validation}).

At the 95th percentile, precision is only 3.1\%, but recall is 37.0\%,
a lift of 7.4 over prevalence. At the 97.5th percentile, precision rises
to 5.5\%, recall is 32.9\%, and lift is 13.1. Those numbers are poor for
automatic labelling but adequate for ranking rare candidates for audit.
The validation is also narrow: the \mention{syn-prl} labels validate a
surface parallelism signal. They don't validate rhetorical function
itself.

The qualitative audit carries the rhetorical-function check. Eighty candidate pairs were
coded by the author in a single-author, non-blind pilot audit: row strata
and scores were visible during coding. Automated reviews flagged
borderline cases for reinspection after coding. The audit yielded 33 yes, 43 no, and 4
uncertain judgments. I use it to identify false-positive classes and
illustrative examples, with no claim of independent interrater
reliability.

High-scoring broad-target candidates performed well: the first batch had
8 yes judgments out of 10 high broad-but-not-narrow items, and the second
had 6 yes judgments out of 6 high broad-target items. By contrast, the
restatement/repetition controls had 0 yes judgments out of 9. Exact
repetition is the strongest surface correlate of formal balance and a
separate false-positive class for this study.

The audit also clarified the boundary of the category. Bare identity,
apposition, dialogue repair, and exact repetition can be rhetorically
relevant apart from isocolon. Conversely, weak length balance can
still matter when the two units share a frame strongly enough to invite
slot substitution. The coding decisions treat isocolon as a
component of a broader rhetorical-balance judgment, with false-positive
classes instead of a mechanically recoverable surface pattern.

\section{Results}

The broad target family shows a positive adjusted increase in composite
formal balance. Across all adjacent pairs, the adjusted effect is 0.0415
with a document-clustered interval from 0.0356 to 0.0474. That's about
0.28 standard deviations of the observed composite score. The subscales
show that the composite effect is driven most strongly by parison: the
adjusted syntax effect is 0.0770, compared with 0.0244 for lexical echo.
With mean-length control only, the broad isocolon effect is 0.0664. The
component split identifies which part of balanced form carries each
signal.

Table~\ref{tab:relation-effects} reports adjusted target
coefficients for the composite and the three components separately.

The broad-family coefficient is an omnibus check. Its strongest labels
are also the labels where co-ordination makes parallel form easiest, so
the cleaner rhetorical test comes from narrower comparisons.

The narrow adversative family is smaller. For
\mention{adversative-antithesis} plus \mention{adversative-contrast}, the
adjusted composite effect is 0.0130, with a confidence interval from
0.0048 to 0.0212.

The individual labels explain the difference.
\mention{joint-disjunction} has an adjusted formal-balance effect of
0.0482, \mention{joint-list} has an adjusted effect of 0.0439, and
\mention{adversative-contrast} has the smaller but more diagnostic
effect of 0.0241.
\mention{adversative-antithesis} is near zero at 0.0039, with an
interval that crosses zero. I read this as a mismatch between a
discourse-relation label and the classical figure. It gives no evidence
against antithesis as a rhetorical resource.
\mention{adversative-concession} is negative at -0.0131.

The relation-level scan also identifies an important positive control.
\mention{restatement-repetition} has the strongest adjusted composite
effect, 0.1902, with especially large lexical and parison effects. That
is exactly what a surface score should find: exact identity is highly
balanced, with lexical echo stronger than the syntactic effect. The human
audit shows why it needs separate treatment.
Repetition can signal clarification, identity, repair, emphasis, or other
rhetorical work; exact repetition remains distinct from balanced
comparison. The analysis treats restatement/repetition as a control
and diagnostic, separate from the comparison hypothesis.

Genre-varying estimates point in the same direction. The broad target
has a partially pooled mean effect of 0.0390 across 20 genres. The
\mention{joint-list} estimate is 0.0398 across 17 genres, and
\mention{adversative-contrast} is 0.0291 across the genres where there
is enough evidence to estimate it. The narrow adversative estimate is
0.0118. The estimates vary across genres, and the broad effect appears
in multiple genres.

\subsection{Robustness checks}

The primary formal-balance results survive document-clustered uncertainty
estimates. With document clustering, the broad-family effect is 0.041
with an interval from 0.036 to 0.047. The \mention{joint-list} effect is
0.044 [0.037, 0.051], \mention{joint-disjunction} is 0.048 [0.030,
0.067], and \mention{adversative-contrast} is 0.024 [0.013, 0.035].
\mention{adversative-antithesis} remains near zero, 0.004 [-0.007,
0.015].

The length-balance result uses a different control specification. Mean
span length and maximum span length nearly determine the length-balance
score, so using both controls suppresses the quantity being estimated.
With mean-length control but without maximum-length control, the
broad-family effect on length balance is 0.066 [0.056, 0.077], and
\mention{adversative-contrast} is 0.073 [0.050, 0.096]. The corresponding
no-length-controls estimates are nearly identical: 0.067 for the broad
family and 0.071 for \mention{adversative-contrast}. The length-balance
claim is reported under mean-length control.

The co-ordination alternative explains part of the pattern. Within
\mention{joint-*} relations only, \mention{joint-list} plus
\mention{joint-disjunction} has a smaller formal-balance effect of 0.017
[0.006, 0.028] against other joint relations. The corresponding parison
effect is 0.029 [0.010, 0.048], while the isocolon effect is only 0.003
[-0.007, 0.013].

Within adversative relations only, \mention{adversative-contrast} remains
positive at 0.030 [0.014, 0.047], again led by parison. These checks give
the co-ordination explanation a direct test. The joint-only baseline
consists of \mention{joint-sequence} and \mention{joint-other}; the
baseline remains multinuclear and partly co-ordinative. The
adversative-only contrast is cleaner. It compares
\mention{adversative-contrast} against other adversative relations.

The stratified permutation check shuffled target labels
500 times within strata defined by document, explicitness,
same-sentence status, colon punctuation, semicolon punctuation, and
quartile of mean word length. The model specification was otherwise the
same as in the main composite analysis. This partial null leaves local
phrase type and dependency-root category unmatched. It tests whether the
result follows only from
the allocation of target labels across documents, lengths, punctuation,
and explicitness. The observed broad-family effect, the
list/disjunction effect, and the adversative-contrast effect all fall
above the 97.5th percentile of their shuffled null distributions
(Table~\ref{tab:stratified-null}).

The composite result is stable across the hand-set weights. Across 66
grid-based nonnegative weightings of length, syntax, and lexical echo,
the broad-family effect is positive with a positive interval in every
specification. For \mention{adversative-contrast}, 65 of 66 grid
specifications are positive and 59 have positive intervals.

\section{Interpretation}

The findings support a restricted version of the rhetorical-function
claim. Balanced form is best treated as a selective way of presenting
units as comparable. It's optional even when comparison is present. That
view predicts an uneven distribution: balance should cluster where
matched presentation has rhetorical value, while remaining absent in many ordinary
cases. The corpus result fits that version, but unevenly. Lists and
disjunctions supply the largest broad-family effects; adversative
contrast supplies the cleaner non-enumerative case.

Here \term{comparable set} is a descriptive cover term for co-membership
relations, including bare co-enumeration. The term allows list structure
without treating every list as comparison for its own sake. The cleanest
non-enumerative signal in the analysis is
\mention{adversative-contrast}.

The component results then refine the claim. The dominant effect is
parison-like more than isocolonic in the narrow length-balance sense. The
evidence is weaker for labels that look rhetorically relevant in the
taxonomy but don't necessarily require balanced presentation in the text.
In particular, \mention{adversative-antithesis} as an eRST relation label
only partly overlaps with antithesis in the classical theory of figures.
The conservative conclusion leaves antithesis itself intact: the corpus label
\mention{adversative-antithesis} is a poor direct proxy
for the classical figure.

That point remains a limitation as well as a finding. The 80-pair audit
contains only eight rows with the \mention{adversative-antithesis} label.
These rows support the label-mismatch interpretation, but they're too few to
separate label mismatch from a second possibility: genuine semantic
antitheses may be expressed through opposition instead of lexical
overlap or surface parallelism. A targeted follow-up should mark
classical antithesis inside the eRST \mention{adversative-antithesis}
set and then test that subset directly.

The composite formal-balance score is associated with a broader act of
placing units into a shared frame. That
frame can be contrastive, but it can also be co-ordinative, disjunctive,
or list-like. In the latter cases, co-ordination itself favours matched
daughters and weakens the rhetorical interpretation. Conversely, some
adversative relations have little balance
because their function is concession, qualification, or discourse
management instead of matched presentation.

Corpus data record ordinary production, not an ideal of trained
rhetorical practice. The present evidence shows where balance appears in
this corpus. It leaves open where balanced form would improve a text.

The co-ordination caveat is substantial. Lists and disjunctions are
places where parallel syntax is grammatically cheap, so the broad effect
cannot be read as a pure rhetorical choice. The joint-only robustness
check shrinks the effect, which is what that objection predicts. The
effect remains positive, and \mention{adversative-contrast} remains
positive inside the adversative family and under a stratified permutation
null. The safer interpretation is that comparable-set relations attract
formal balance beyond genre, document, length, and punctuation
differences, while much of that balance follows the syntax of
co-ordination.

The pattern also clarifies the scope of the corpus study. The evidence is
textual and distributional. It concerns where balanced form occurs, not
how readers experience those pairs. A high score by itself still falls
short of a rhetorical-figure label. The analysis shows that a graded
measure of formal balance is systematically
higher in particular discourse-relation contexts. The human audit
illustrates why many high-scoring cases are rhetorically relevant and why
this remains pilot evidence, pending an independently validated
annotation layer.

The RST Signalling Corpus will add a later triangulation point once the
data is available. Work on discourse signalling has shown that coherence
relations are often signalled by more than discourse markers, including
lexical, syntactic, graphical, semantic, and genre-based cues. That point
is developed in the original signalling annotation work and in later
studies of RST signals and multiple signals
\citep{taboadaDas2013AnnotationUponAnnotation,dasTaboada2018RSTSignallingCorpus,dasTaboada2018SignallingBeyondMarkers,dasTaboada2019MultipleSignals}.
This literature gives the project a stronger comparative baseline. If
formal balance behaves like a discourse signal, then it should be
testable alongside other signal types as well as an isolated figure
score.

\section{Conclusion}

A corpus study cannot prove that isocolon makes readers perceive
comparison as more forceful, and it leaves rhetorical pedagogy untested.
It can test a cleaner textual claim: writers use formal balance unevenly
across discourse relations, and the relations most associated with
comparable-set construals should show more of it. The present results
support that claim for a broad family of comparison-like and
co-ordinative relations, with the largest effects in lists and
disjunctions and the cleaner non-enumerative effect in adversative
contrast.

The analysis gives selective support to the inherited figure label
\term{antithesis}. The narrow adversative effect is small, and antithesis
as a discourse-relation label is close to zero. That finding sharpens the
mapping between relation labels and classical figures. The strongest
signal is parison-like parallelism; isocolon narrowly understood as
length balance contributes less to the main composite effect. Balanced
form remains a rhetorical resource, and the
present corpus evidence supports a claim about comparable-set construals
and formal balance more securely than a claim about equal cola alone.

The robustness checks sharpen that conclusion. Length balance appears
under mean-length conditioning without maximum length, while the main
composite model is led by parison. The joint-only comparison also shows
that a large share of the broad effect reflects co-ordination itself. The
stratified permutation check places the observed effects above document,
length, punctuation, and explicitness-matched nulls. What remains is a
modest, distributional claim: comparable-set relations prefer matched
form in ordinary corpus production, and the clearest measurable form in
this corpus is syntactic parallelism.
